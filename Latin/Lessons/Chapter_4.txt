    # What is the Dative Case?

    The dative case is used to show the indirect object in a sentence, which is the person or thing receiving the action indirectly. It answers the question, "To whom?" or "For whom?"

     - Example: “He gives a gift to the girl.”

    In Latin, the indirect object changes its ending to show the dative case.

    # Dative Endings for the First and Second Declensions

    Here are the dative singular endings for the first and second declensions:

     - First Declension: -ae (e.g., puellae – "to/for the girl")
     - Second Declension: -o (e.g., amico – "to/for the friend")

    # Examples of the Dative Case in Sentences

    Here are some examples using the dative case as the indirect object:

     - Puellae donum dat – He/she gives a gift to the girl.
     - Servo cibum parat – He/she prepares food for the servant.
     - Amico fabulam narrat – He/she tells a story to the friend.

    In each example, the noun in the dative case indicates who is receiving the action indirectly.

    # Practice with Direct and Indirect Objects

Practice creating sentences that include both a direct object (in the accusative case) and an indirect object (in the dative case). This helps you build more complete sentences in Latin.

    # Quid est Casus Dativus?

    Casus dativus adhibetur ad objectum indirectum ostendendum, id est, persona vel res quae actionem indirecte recipit. Respondet quaestionibus, “Cui?” vel “Pro quo?”

     - Exemplum: “Donum puellae dat.”

    In Latina, objectum indirectum finem mutat ut casum dativum indicet.

    # Fines Dativi Primae et Secundae Declinationis

    Hic sunt fines dativi singularis primae et secundae declinationis:

     - Prima Declinatione: -ae (ex. puellae – "to/for the girl")
     - Secunda Declinatione: -o (ex. amico – "to/for the friend")

    # Exempla Casus Dativi in Sententiis

    Hic sunt quaedam exempla ubi casus dativus ut objectum indirectum adhibetur:

     - Puellae donum dat
     - Servo cibum parat
     - Amico fabulam narrat

    In singulis exemplis, nomen in casu dativo indicat cui indirecte actio praestatur.

    # Exercitatio cum Objectis Directis et Indirectis

    Exerceamus sententias creandas quae et objectum directum (casus accusativus) et objectum indirectum (casus dativus) habeant. Hoc modo sententias plenas in lingua Latina formare discimus.
