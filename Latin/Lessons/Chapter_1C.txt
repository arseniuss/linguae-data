	1. Quid sunt Pronomina?

	Pronomen est verbum quod pro nomine ponitur. In Anglica, utimur pronominibus sicut "ego," "tu," "is," "ea," et "id." Pronomina nos adiuvant ne nomina repetamus.

	2. In Latina, pronomina etiam suffixa mutant secundum suam partem in sententia (subiectum, obiectum, etc.). Hic sunt pronomina personalia singularia:

	 - ego – ego (nominativus, forma subiectiva)
	 - mei – mei (genitivus, forma possessiva)
	 - tu – tu (nominativus, forma subiectiva)
	 - tui – tui (genitivus, forma possessiva)
	 - is, ea, id – is, ea, id (nominativus, forma subiectiva)

	Haec pronomina nobis permittunt ut simplices sententias sine specificis nominibus creemus.

	3. Nominibus et Pronominibus in Sententiis Coniungendis

	Cum pronominibus nunc cognitis, eas cum nominibus et verbis coniungere possumus ut varias sententias faciamus:

    	Ego amicus sum – Ego amicus sum.
	 - Ego (ego) est pronomen subiectivum in casu nominativo.
	 - Amicus (amicus) describit subiectum.
	 - Sum significat "ego sum."

	Tu puellam amas – Tu puellam amas.
	 - Tu (tu) est pronomen subiectivum in casu nominativo.
	 - Puellam (puellam) est obiectum in casu accusativo.

	4. Exercitatio Pronominibus in Sententiis Utendis

	Pronomina nobis plures optiones pro varietate sententiarum dant. Exercitatio cum simplicibus sententiis nos adiuvat ad ordinem verborum Latinorum et casus intellegendos.

	1. What are Pronouns?

	A pronoun is a word used in place of a noun. In English, we use pronouns like "I," "you," "he," "she," and "it." Pronouns help us avoid repeating nouns.

	2. Personal Pronouns in Latin

	In Latin, pronouns also change their endings based on their role in the sentence (subject, object, etc.). Here are the basic singular pronouns:

	 - ego – I (nominative, subject form)
	 - mei – of me (genitive, possessive form)
	 - tu – you (nominative, subject form)
	 - tui – of you (genitive, possessive form)
	 - is, ea, id – he, she, it (nominative, subject form)

	These pronouns allow us to create simple sentences without using specific names.

	3. Combining Nouns and Pronouns in Sentences

	Now that we know some pronouns, we can combine them with nouns and verbs to make different types of sentences:

    	Ego amicus sum – I am a friend.

	 - Ego (I) is the subject pronoun in nominative case.
	 - Amicus (friend) describes the subject.
	 - Sum means "I am."

	Tu puellam amas – You love the girl.

	 - Tu (you) is the subject pronoun in nominative case.
	 - Puellam (the girl) is the object in accusative case.
	 - Amas means "you love."

	4. Practice Using Pronouns in Sentences

	Pronouns give us more options for sentence variety. Practicing with simple sentences helps us become comfortable with Latin word order and cases.
